#Let’s revisit our previous adventures with college football play-by-play and try a variation on our exploration of first-play interceptions:

#Are teams passing more on first down this season than last season? We know that there are more teams in this year’s play-by-play data than last years, so what we want to measure is this: on a percentage basis, are more first down plays passes this season?

#You should answer that using as many seasons as we have data for, defining what you mean by first-down passes both in code and in the text accompanying it. Make a line chart showing all the teams’ first-down pass percentage by season.

#get all first down pass plays
pass_first <- pbp %>%
  filter(pass == 1, down == 1) %>%
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>%
  summarise(count = n())
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'season'. You can override using the
## `.groups` argument.
#get all first down plays
all_plays <- pbp %>%
  filter(down == 1) %>%
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>%
  summarise(total = n())
## `summarise()` has grouped output by 'season'. You can override using the
## `.groups` argument.
#combine pass-first and all plays into one dataframe with a percentage
combined_first_down_plays <- inner_join(pass_first, all_plays, by = c('pos_team', 'season')) %>%
  filter(count > 10) %>%
  mutate(percent = (count/total)*100)
#plotting a line chart showing all the teams' first-down pass percentage by season.
ggplot() + geom_line(data=combined_first_down_plays, aes(x=season, y=percent, group=pos_team))

So, what can this graph tell us…? Not much. The one thing I do want to point out, though, is the overall trend since 2014 of teams passing on first down seems to be going down. If we look at this line and try to pick out one trend, we see a bit of a downward slide. This is an interesting finding because it seems like modern-day college offenses are pass-first, but this graph questions that assertion to a degree.

There is something to be said for knowing who you are as a team and sticking to it. Starting with Purdue, the Boilermakers are a team that have consistently been above Big Ten average over the last nine years when it comes to passing on first down. If we look at their trendline, even though Purdue has passed the ball less on first down this season, they still opt to throw more than conference average, a testament to how much they like to give their quarterback the green light.

On the opposite side of the spectrum is Wisconsin, which has been below division average according to the data we have from 2014. Wisconsin did have RB Jonathan Taylor, the Big Ten’s top running back for several years in the mid 2010s, and as a team, their organizational philosophy has been one of hand the ball off and run, so this checks out.

Let’s now take a look at Maryland. We see a massive spike in 2018 where the team went from passing on first down about 20% of the time to about 35% the following year. And every season since, the Terps have either thrown the ball more or been well above the conference average. Why? Mike Locksley was hired in December of 2018 from Alabama and was tasked with rejuvenating a tired Maryland football program. An offensive coordinator at ’Bama, Locksley has spent most of his time at Maryland with a high-level quarterback in Taulia Tagovailoa, and our graph reflects the team’s willingness to pass on first down.

I think we can also look at teams that have recently struggled on offense and look at their pass plays on first down. Iowa, Minnesota and Rutgers all rank below the conference average, and none of these teams have been known to have lights-out offenses, and Iowa is the only school of the three to have been competitive in recent years. Then you look at a team like Ohio State — annually in the CFP conversation — and they also haven’t been that high above league average, if at all. But, the Buckeyes have had some exceptional runners in recent years, so I think this chart tells us more about what teams do on first down (pass or run) and less about how that impacts the strength of a team. In other words, just cause a team throws the ball more on first down doesn’t mean it is one of the Big Ten’s top units.

A few other notes about this data. The data that we have from the cfbfastR isn’t the most reliable. For example, when filtering to create a dataframe that contained just the Big Ten teams, there were a number of other schools included that do not play in the conference. This made sorting through for just Big Ten teams difficult. What wound up helping was merging the two dataframes — pass plays and all plays — which gave us just the Big Ten schools.

The other issue was that we got hundreds of NA results, likely errors in tracking the data when the information was entered into the system. To fix this, we grouped by year instead of season, which gave us more precise numbers.
